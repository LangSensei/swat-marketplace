# Cost and Energy Structure Reference

## Use Notes

- All values below are **indicative ranges**, not quotations.
- Actual numbers vary by region, energy price, batch size, machine loading, dye class, wastewater burden, and mill efficiency.
- Use this file to frame trade-offs, not to pretend there is one universal market price.

## Piece-Dyeing Cost Structure for Woven Synthetics

| Cost block | Typical range, USD/kg fabric | What moves it most |
|---|---:|---|
| Dyes | 0.15-1.20 | Shade depth, dye class, fastness target, brand tier |
| Auxiliaries | 0.08-0.45 | Leveling, dispersing, anti-crease, reduction clearing, aftertreatment |
| Steam or thermal utilities | 0.15-0.60 | Peak temperature, hold time, liquor ratio, drying load |
| Electricity | 0.03-0.18 | Pumping, circulation, drying air, finishing line load |
| Labor and QC | 0.08-0.30 | Batch size, rework rate, inspection intensity |
| Overhead, wastewater, and compliance burden | 0.10-0.50 | Water treatment intensity, sludge cost, plant overhead |

## Water Consumption Reference

| Process step | Typical water use, L/kg fabric | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| Scouring or initial washing | 8-20 | Depends on oil load and cleanliness target |
| Exhaust dyeing main bath | 20-50 | Liquor ratio is the biggest lever |
| Rinsing and afterwashing | 10-30 | Deep shades and reduction clearing push this upward |
| Reduction clearing or strong aftercleaning | 3-10 | Mainly PET-family medium and deep shades |
| Padding and finishing application | 2-8 | Lower water burden than exhaust routes, but drying and cure still cost energy |

## Energy Reference

| Process step | Thermal energy, MJ/kg fabric | Electricity, kWh/kg fabric | Notes |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Heat-up and peak hold for exhaust dyeing | 4-10 | 0.10-0.35 | Higher for PET at deep shades and long dwell |
| Rinsing and afterwash heating | 1-4 | 0.03-0.12 | Grows with wash count and water volume |
| Drying and curing after finishing | 2-6 | 0.10-0.40 | Wide spread depending on wet pickup and stenter efficiency |
| Coating or multi-pass finishing routes | 3-8 | 0.15-0.50 | Strongly line-speed dependent |

## What Usually Moves Cost the Most

1. **Shade depth** - deeper shades increase dye cost, wash burden, and rework sensitivity.
2. **Liquor ratio** - it drives both water and thermal cost in exhaust processing.
3. **Rework rate** - one correction bath can erase a large margin advantage.
4. **Durability claims** - stronger fastness or DWR durability usually means more chemistry, more aftercleaning, or tighter cure control.
5. **Wastewater burden** - reductions in water load can matter as much as the chemistry price itself.

## Practical Trade-Off Reminders

- Moving from PET to a lower-temperature polyester pathway can reduce thermal burden, but levelness and fastness risk may rise.
- Fluorine-free DWR can improve compliance position while increasing the probability of performance compromises on oil repellency or wash retention.
- A lower chemistry cost is not automatically cheaper if it raises rework, wash count, or customer-claim risk.
