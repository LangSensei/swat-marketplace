# Fiber Identification Quick Reference

## Use Notes

- Treat all values here as practical screening ranges, not certificate-grade identity proof.
- For buyer-side decisions, use at least two independent clues before calling a fiber family with high confidence.
- DSC, density, and solubility usually separate close candidates better than burn smell alone.

## Comparative Table

| Fiber | Burn behavior | DSC melting point, C | Density, g/cm3 | FTIR cues | Solubility clues | Practical implication |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|---|---|
| PA6 | Melts, draws, blue flame with orange tip, hard tan bead, celery-like or protein-like odor | 220-223 | 1.13-1.15 | Amide bands near 3300, 1630, 1535 cm-1 | Dissolves or softens relatively quickly in formic acid; soluble in m-cresol | Easier dye uptake than PA66, lower thermal ceiling, more moisture-sensitive |
| PA66 | Similar to PA6 but slightly tougher bead and slower melting response | 255-262 | 1.14-1.16 | Polyamide pattern similar to PA6; use DSC more than FTIR to split them | Formic-acid response is slower than PA6; soluble in m-cresol | Better heat resistance than PA6, often slower dye diffusion |
| PET | Shrinks then melts, sooty flame, hard black bead, sweet aromatic odor | 250-258 | 1.38-1.40 | Ester carbonyl near 1712-1718 cm-1 and aromatic ester pattern | Insoluble in formic acid; attacked only by strong specialty solvents | Standard disperse-dye polyester; highest common temperature demand |
| PBT | Polyester-like burn but softer and faster melting than PET | 223-229 | 1.30-1.32 | Polyester-like FTIR; DSC is more useful than FTIR for separation | Insoluble in formic acid; polyester-type solvent behavior | Lower dyeing window than PET, softer hand, easier thermal damage if overrun |
| CDP | Similar to PET in burn behavior | 220-240 | 1.35-1.39 | PET-like ester pattern with modified polyester behavior; weak sulfonate-related features may appear | Insoluble in formic acid | Cationic-dyeable polyester with lower thermal stability than standard PET |
| Spandex or elastane | Shrinks away from flame, soft black residue, acrid odor, no clean hard bead | Broad softening or decomposition rather than one sharp melt | 1.20-1.23 | Urethane or urea features near 3320, 1725, 1530 cm-1 | Often swells or partially dissolves in DMF; unstable in strong acids and chlorine systems | Usually the thermal limit in stretch fabrics; protect during dyeing and finishing |
| Sorona or PTT family | Polyester-like burn, softer bead than PET | 225-232 | 1.31-1.33 | Polyester-type spectrum | Insoluble in formic acid | Lower-temperature disperse behavior than PET, softer resilience, better stretch recovery than plain PET |
| Elastic bicomponent polyester family (T-400 or T-800 type) | Polyester-like burn response; behavior depends on component mix | Dual or broadened peaks, often around 220-255 | 1.32-1.38 | Polyester-like FTIR often cannot identify the exact trade family | Insoluble in formic acid | Use supplier disclosure plus DSC, because bicomponent stretch systems can look like ordinary polyester in simple screening tests |

## Fast Decision Tree

1. **Amide bands plus formic-acid response** -> start with the polyamide branch.
2. **Ester carbonyl and no formic-acid response** -> start with the polyester branch.
3. **Broad softening, DMF sensitivity, and strong elastic recovery** -> suspect spandex/elastane content.
4. **Polyamide branch:** DSC near 220 C points to PA6; DSC near 255-262 C points to PA66.
5. **Polyester branch:** cationic dyeability and lower thermal stability suggest CDP; lower melt range and softer handle suggest PBT or PTT-family materials; standard PET usually sits highest in melt range.
6. **Dual or broadened DSC peaks** plus stretch behavior suggest bicomponent polyester families such as T-400 or T-800.

## Practical Warnings

- FTIR alone is rarely enough to separate PET, PBT, CDP, and elastic bicomponent polyesters.
- Burn tests are good for ruling out fiber families, not for splitting close relatives with high confidence.
- Trade names such as Sorona, T-400, and T-800 should be checked against supplier technical data whenever the commercial decision matters.
