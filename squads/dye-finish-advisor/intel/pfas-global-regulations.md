# PFAS Global Regulations

## Update Note

- Last updated: 2026-04-29
- Use this file as a market-screening reference, not as a substitute for the current legal text.
- For textiles, always separate **finished article restrictions**, **supplier declarations**, and **MRSL or process-chemistry controls**.

## Interpretation Rules

1. **Intentionally added PFAS** is a legal trigger in several US state laws even when no numeric article threshold is given.
2. **TOF or total fluorine** is a screening tool, not a full substance list. A low TOF screen supports a PFAS-free position; it does not replace analyte-specific testing when a regulation or claim requires it.
3. **ZDHC MRSL** applies to manufacturing chemical formulations and process inputs, not directly to finished fabric articles.
4. **OEKO-TEX** is a product-certification scheme; it is commercially important but not the same as a national law.

## Practical Reading of the Table

- **California** is the cleanest finished-goods reference for mainstream textile articles because it combines an intentionally added ban with a TOF screen.
- **EU listed-PFAS rows** are important for legal residue management even when the brand strategy is already "PFAS-free".
- **OEKO-TEX** and **ZDHC** are often used together: OEKO-TEX for product-facing confidence, ZDHC for process-input control.
- **New York, Washington, and Minnesota** should be read as strong substitution signals even when the category wording is narrower or more phased than California.
